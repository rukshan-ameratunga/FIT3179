Weather patterns across Australia

The visualisation presented has been constructed using weather data from Australia over the years following the Millenium Drought in the 2000s, Various facets of what makes up weather patterns has been presented in order to give the viewer a better understanding of the science behind the physical phenoma we often take for granted.

Relative humidity is given as the amount of moisture in the air as a percentage of the amount the air can actually hold. Warmer air is able to hold greater moisture than colder air, this means keeping atmospheric pressure constant, the relative humidity will be lesser in warm air than in cold air. This negative correlation is present across all States and Territories shown. Rain is also related to the water content in the air; humidity is at 100% when it rains. The clouds are unable to hold any more water. This phenomena is particularly clear in WA and SA, heavier rains are experienced at higher relative humidity. When the air is hotter, evaporation takes place, humidity increases and it rains, once the air has lost its water content, the temperature drops and we experience what is known as a "cool change".

Average rainfall is Australia is considered to be quite low, between 81 and 250 mm. Southern areas of the land experience rain-bearing cold spells when high-pressure systems move toward to the north during Winter months. Monsoon season during the summer brings about much wet weather to the north, this is evident in QLD and NT. Rainfall is typically concentrated along the eastern coastal regions (NSW, VIC, and parts of QLD). 2015 was a particularly poor year, being 5% below the 1961-1990 climatological average of 465.2 mm. This is a 30 year average of a weather variable, namely rainfall, traditionally serving as the accepted norm by meteorologists.
